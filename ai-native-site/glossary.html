Glossary of AI Terms

Plain-English definitions of the terms you'll encounter when working with AI tools and reading about AI at work. Listed alphabetically.

No matching terms found.

Agent
An AI system that can plan and take actions autonomously, using tools like web search or code execution to complete multi-step tasks with minimal oversight.
Agentic workflow
A task sequence where AI decisions and outputs feed into the next step automatically, requiring little human input between actions. Useful for complex, multi-stage processes.
Alignment
The degree to which an AI system's behavior matches human intentions, values, and goals. Misalignment is when the model does something technically correct but not what you actually wanted.
API
Application Programming Interface. The technical bridge that lets software systems talk to each other. AI APIs let developers embed model capabilities directly into apps and workflows.
Artifact
The specific output you're asking AI to produce: a document, email, table, summary, or other deliverable. Defining it clearly before prompting improves quality.
Attention mechanism
The core innovation inside transformer models. It allows the model to weigh how relevant each word in the input is to every other word — enabling nuanced understanding of context.
Benchmark
A standardized test used to measure and compare AI model capabilities — reasoning, coding, factual accuracy, safety, etc. Useful for comparing models but not a complete picture of real-world performance.
Chain-of-thought
A prompting technique that asks the AI to reason through a problem step by step before giving a final answer. Improves accuracy on complex or multi-part tasks.
Chunking
Breaking large documents into smaller, overlapping segments before feeding them into an AI model or vector database. Necessary because models have context limits and retrieve information in pieces.
Constitutional AI
Anthropic's approach to training AI systems using a set of explicit principles (a "constitution") that the model uses to critique and revise its own outputs during training.
Context collapse
When important earlier information in a conversation is effectively forgotten or deprioritized as the context window fills up. Longer conversations can cause models to lose track of early instructions.
Context window
The maximum amount of text a model can process in one interaction—your input plus its output combined. Most frontier models now handle 100,000+ tokens.
Embedding
A numerical representation of text that captures semantic meaning. Used to power semantic search, document retrieval, and similarity matching in AI systems.
Emergent behavior
Capabilities that appear in larger models that were not explicitly trained for and were not present in smaller versions of the same model. Emergent abilities are difficult to predict in advance.
Evals (Evaluation)
Systematic tests used to measure AI model outputs for quality, accuracy, safety, or reliability. Well-designed evals are the gold standard for knowing whether an AI system actually works for your use case.
Few-shot prompting
Providing 2–5 examples inside your prompt to show the model the exact format, tone, or structure you want in the output. More reliable than describing the format in words.
Fine-tuning
Training an existing model on a curated dataset to specialize its behavior for a particular domain, task, or style. Requires technical infrastructure and labeled data.
Foundation model
A large AI model trained on broad data (like Claude, GPT-4, or Gemini) that can be applied to many tasks without retraining. The base most AI products are built on.
Generative AI
AI systems that produce new content — text, images, code, audio, video — rather than simply classifying or predicting from existing data. The category that includes LLMs, image generators, and voice models.
Grounding
Connecting AI responses to specific verified documents or data sources. Reduces hallucination by anchoring the model to facts rather than relying on training alone.
Guardrails
Constraints built into an AI system to prevent harmful, off-topic, or policy-violating outputs. Can be implemented in the model itself, through system prompts, or via output filtering layers.
Hallucination
When an AI confidently generates false, fabricated, or inaccurate information that sounds plausible. The most important failure mode to verify against before using AI output.
Human-in-the-loop
A workflow design where a human reviews, approves, or corrects AI outputs before they are acted upon. Essential in high-stakes contexts where errors are costly or hard to reverse.
Inference
The process of an AI model generating a response from its training. Inference cost and speed vary by model size—larger models are slower and more expensive to run.
Instruction following
A model's ability to accurately carry out specific directives in a prompt. Stronger instruction-following means the model does what you say, even on nuanced or multi-step tasks.
Knowledge cutoff
The date after which a model has no training data. Events, publications, or changes after this date are unknown to the model unless provided in the prompt. Always check cutoff dates for time-sensitive topics.
Latency
The time it takes for a model to begin or complete a response. Larger models typically have higher latency. Important consideration when integrating AI into real-time or user-facing workflows.
LLM
Large Language Model. An AI model trained on vast amounts of text to understand and generate natural language. The core technology behind most conversational AI tools.
Model
A trained AI system with specific capabilities, a defined size (measured in parameters), and distinct behavioral characteristics. Different models — even from the same provider — behave differently and suit different tasks.
Multimodal
AI systems that can process multiple input types—text, images, audio, or video—in a single model. GPT-4o, Claude, and Gemini are all multimodal models.
Neural network
The computational architecture underlying modern AI models. Loosely inspired by the brain, it consists of layers of mathematical operations that learn patterns from training data.
Orchestration
Coordinating multiple AI models, tools, or agents to complete a complex task. An orchestration layer routes inputs, manages outputs, and chains steps together — often invisibly to the end user.
Parameters
The numerical weights inside a model that encode everything it has learned during training. Model size (e.g., "7B" or "70B") refers to how many billions of parameters the model has.
Persona
A defined role or character given to an AI via a system prompt to shape its communication style, expertise, or perspective. Useful for creating consistent product experiences or tailored assistants.
Prompt
The instruction, question, or input you give to an AI model to guide its response. Prompt quality is the primary lever for output quality in everyday AI use.
Prompt injection
An attack where malicious instructions are embedded in content the AI reads — a document, email, or web page — causing it to override its original instructions. A key security risk in agentic workflows.
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Relevant documents are fetched and added to the AI's context before it responds, grounding answers in real data rather than training memory.
Reasoning model
AI models (like o1 or Claude's extended thinking mode) that generate an internal chain of reasoning before producing a final answer. Slower and more expensive, but substantially more accurate on hard problems.
Role prompting
Assigning the AI a specific role or area of expertise in your prompt ("Act as a senior editor…") to shape the style, depth, and focus of its response.
Semantic search
Search that retrieves results based on conceptual meaning rather than exact keyword matches. Powered by embeddings — finds "heart attack symptoms" even if the document says "myocardial infarction."
Structured output
AI responses formatted as JSON, tables, or other machine-readable formats rather than prose. Essential when AI output needs to feed into another system or workflow step without manual parsing.
System prompt
Instructions set before a conversation begins that shape the AI's behavior, persona, or constraints throughout the session. Used in products to define tone and scope.
Temperature
A parameter controlling output randomness. Low values produce focused, consistent responses. High values produce more varied or creative ones. Most tools set this automatically.
Token
The basic unit an LLM processes—roughly ¾ of a word on average. Context limits, API costs, and processing speed are all measured in tokens.
Tool use
The ability of an AI model to call external functions—web search, code execution, calendar access, APIs—during a conversation to retrieve live information or take action.
Transformer
The neural network architecture that powers virtually all modern LLMs. Introduced in the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" paper, it enables models to process text in parallel and capture long-range context.
Vector database
A database optimized for storing and searching high-dimensional embedding vectors. The storage layer that makes RAG and semantic search fast at scale. Examples: Pinecone, Weaviate, pgvector.
Workflow automation
Using AI to execute sequences of tasks — drafting, classifying, routing, summarizing — with minimal human input between steps. The practical goal of most enterprise AI adoption.
Zero-shot
Asking an AI to perform a task without providing any examples, relying entirely on the instruction and its training. Works well for common tasks; few-shot helps for unusual formats.
