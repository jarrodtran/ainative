Polish Existing Text

Subject Line or Headline Options

The content is about: [brief description] Audience: [who will read it] Goal: [what the reader should do or feel — click, open, understand, act] Give a range: some short and direct, some curiosity-driven, some that lead with the benefit.

What, if anything, they need to decide or do Keep it under [150 / 200 / 300] words. No jargon. Document: [paste here]
Writing
Difficult Message
Help me write a message that delivers difficult news. Don't soften the key message to the point of obscuring it.

Key details: - What is changing: [describe] - When it takes effect: [date or timeline] - Why it's happening: [brief rationale] - What people need to do (if anything): [action required] Tone: [matter-of-fact / enthusiastic / reassuring] Length: [1–2 short paragraphs / under 200 words]
Research & Summarization
Research
Research Brief
I need to understand [topic] for [specific purpose]. Give me: 1. The core concept in plain English (2–3 sentences) 2. Three key things I need to know 3. Main risks or misconceptions to watch for 4. One question I should ask before relying on this I am not a technical expert — keep it practical.
Research
Action-Oriented Summary
Summarize this document for [audience] who needs to [make a decision / take action]. Include: - The key finding in one sentence - What it means for [audience] - Recommended next step - What is still uncertain or unknown Source: [paste document here]
Research
Explain It Plainly
Explain [concept / topic / term] to someone who works in [my field] but has no background in [the subject area]. Use a concrete analogy or example from everyday work. Avoid jargon. If a technical term is unavoidable, define it in plain language. End with the one thing they most need to remember.
Research
Extract Key Points
Read this [article / report / transcript] and extract: 1. The 5 most important points 2. Any data or statistics worth keeping 3. Quotes I might want to cite 4. Questions this raises that aren't answered Be selective — only include what is genuinely significant. Source: [paste here]
Research
Fact-Check This
Review this text and flag any claims that: - Are factually questionable or likely outdated - Rely on assumptions I should verify - Would embarrass me if they turned out to be wrong For each flagged item, explain why it's worth checking and what I should verify. Text: [paste here]
Research
Turn Notes Into a Draft
Turn these rough notes into a coherent [document / summary / briefing]. Do not invent information — only use what's in the notes. If something is unclear or incomplete, flag it with [NEEDS INPUT] rather than guessing. Format: [bullet list / short paragraphs / structured sections with headers] Notes: [paste here]
Analysis & Decision-Making
Analysis
Compare Options
Compare [option A] and [option B] for [use case]. Create a comparison table with these criteria: - [criterion 1] - [criterion 2] - [criterion 3] Then give a recommendation based on: [my priorities or constraints]. Note any trade-offs I should think through before deciding.
Analysis
Risk Check
Review this [plan / proposal / decision] and identify: 1. Risks I might have missed 2. Assumptions that could be wrong 3. Questions a skeptic would raise 4. One thing to verify before moving forward [paste here]
Analysis
Devil's Advocate
I'm about to [make a decision / launch a plan / send a proposal]. Play devil's advocate. Make the strongest possible case against it. Don't soften the critique — I need to know the real objections before someone else raises them. My plan: [describe here]
Analysis
Root Cause Analysis
Help me identify the root cause of this problem. Problem: [describe the symptom or failure] What we know: [any data, patterns, or context] What we've already ruled out: [attempted fixes or eliminated causes] Use the "5 Whys" approach or another structured method. Give me 2–3 plausible root causes ranked by likelihood, with the evidence that supports each.
Analysis
What Am I Missing?
I've been thinking about [problem / plan / decision] and here's where I've landed: [describe your current thinking] What important angles, trade-offs, or second-order effects might I be overlooking? Be specific — I don't need general frameworks, I need gaps in my actual reasoning.
Analysis
Prioritize This List
Help me prioritize this list of [tasks / projects / initiatives]. Criteria that matter most to me: [e.g., impact, effort, urgency, dependencies, cost] Context: [any constraints — team size, deadline, budget, strategic goal] My list: - [item 1] - [item 2] - [item 3] Suggest a ranked order with a brief reason for each placement. Flag anything that should be cut or deferred.
Meetings & Collaboration
Meetings
Meeting Recap
Turn these meeting notes into a structured recap. Include: 1. Key decisions made (with brief context) 2. Action items (owner and deadline where mentioned) 3. Open questions or unresolved items 4. Recommended next steps Notes: [paste notes here]
Meetings
Meeting Agenda
Build a meeting agenda for a [X]-minute [meeting type: kickoff / review / planning / retrospective]. Goal of the meeting: [what we need to leave with — a decision, alignment, a plan, etc.] Attendees: [who's coming and their role] Known topics to cover: - [topic 1] - [topic 2] Include time allocations and flag which items require a decision vs. are informational.
Meetings
Difficult Conversation Prep
Help me prepare for a difficult conversation. Situation: [what the conversation is about] Who I'm talking to: [role, relationship, what they care about] My goal: [what I want to happen as a result] What I'm worried about: [likely objections, emotional reactions, or ways it could go wrong] Give me: the key points to make, how to open the conversation, and how to handle the most likely pushback.
Meetings
Status Update
Write a weekly status update for [project / team / stakeholder]. This week: - Completed: [what got done] - In progress: [what's underway] - Blocked: [any blockers and what's needed to unblock] - Coming next week: [planned work] Tone: [concise / reassuring / direct] Length: [3–5 bullet points per section / under 200 words total]
Meetings
Talking Points
Give me talking points for [presentation / conversation / call] with [audience]. My goal: [what I want them to decide, understand, or do] Key message: [the one thing I must land] Their likely concerns: [what they'll push back on or ask about] What I should avoid: [topics, framing, or language to stay away from] Format as: opening line, 3–4 supporting points, closing ask.
Meetings
Retrospective
Run a retrospective on [project / quarter / sprint]. Using this input from the team: [paste notes, survey responses, or your own observations] Structure the output as: - What went well (and why it worked) - What didn't go well (root cause, not just symptoms) - What we'd do differently - 2–3 concrete changes to make going forward Be honest. Don't just validate — identify real patterns.
Presentations & Storytelling
Presentations
Slide Outline
Create a slide-by-slide outline for a [X]-slide presentation on [topic]. Audience: [who's in the room and what they care about] Goal: [what they should think, feel, or do after] Key message: [the one thing this presentation must land] Time available: [X minutes] For each slide: give a title, 2–3 bullet points of content, and the point it's making in the narrative arc.
Presentations
One-Pager
Write a one-pager on [topic / initiative / proposal]. Sections to include: - What it is (2–3 sentences) - Why it matters (the problem it solves) - How it works (key details, no jargon) - What's needed (resources, decisions, or next steps) Audience: [who will read this] Tone: [persuasive / informational / factual] Length: Fit on one page. Be ruthless with every word.
Presentations
Narrative Arc
Help me build a narrative arc for this presentation. Topic: [what it's about] Audience: [who they are and what they already believe] Current situation: [what's true now — the problem or tension] Desired outcome: [what I want the audience to agree to or do] Structure the story as: situation → complication → resolution → call to action. Give me the key beat for each stage.
Presentations
Stakeholder Brief
Write a briefing document for [stakeholder / executive / committee] about [topic]. They need to know: - Background (1 short paragraph — assume they know the business, not the specifics) - Current state (what's happening now) - Key decision or ask (what you need from them) - Options considered (if relevant) - Recommendation and rationale Keep it scannable. Lead with the ask, not the history.
Strategy & Planning
Strategy
Frame the Problem
Help me frame this problem before I try to solve it. What's happening: [describe the situation] What I think the problem is: [my current framing] Challenge my framing. Offer 2–3 alternative ways to define the problem that might lead to different (and better) solutions. For each reframe, note what solution it would suggest.
Strategy
Project Kickoff Brief
Write a project kickoff brief for [project name]. Include: - Objective (what success looks like) - Scope (what's in and explicitly what's out) - Key stakeholders and their roles - Milestones and rough timeline - Risks and open questions - Definition of done Keep it to one page. Use plain language — this will be shared with the whole team.
Strategy
Goals to Action Plan
Turn this goal into a concrete action plan. Goal: [describe the outcome you're trying to reach] Timeline: [when it needs to be done] Constraints: [team size, budget, dependencies, or other limits] Break it into: phases or milestones, specific tasks within each, owners if I name the team, and decision points where the plan might change. Flag any assumptions baked into the plan.
Strategy
Pre-Mortem
Run a pre-mortem on this plan. Imagine it's 6 months from now and the plan failed. What went wrong? My plan: [describe here] Give me the top 5 most likely failure modes — not edge cases, but realistic ways this goes sideways. For each: what caused it, what the early warning sign would have been, and what we could do now to prevent it.
Strategy
SWOT in Plain Language
Do a plain-language SWOT analysis for [organization / product / initiative / decision]. Context: [brief description of the situation] For each quadrant, give 3–5 specific points — not generic observations. Avoid obvious ones. Prioritize what's actually decision-relevant. End with: the single biggest opportunity to act on and the single biggest risk to address.
Strategy
Build the Business Case
Help me build a business case for [investment / initiative / change]. What I'm proposing: [describe] What problem it solves: [the pain or opportunity] Expected benefits: [quantify where possible — time saved, revenue, risk reduced] Cost or effort: [budget, headcount, time] Alternatives considered: [what else could be done] Structure it for a [senior leader / finance audience / operational team] who will want to know: is this worth it, what's the risk, and when do we see return?
Feedback & Review
Feedback
Review This Document
Review this document as a [target reader — e.g., skeptical executive / new hire / technical reviewer]. Tell me: 1. What landed well 2. What confused you or felt unconvincing 3. What's missing that you expected to see 4. One thing that would make this significantly stronger Be direct. Don't just validate — I need honest feedback. Document: [paste here]
Feedback
Give Constructive Feedback
Help me write constructive feedback on [this work / this performance / this deliverable]. Context: [who it's for and what the work was] What I genuinely appreciated: [specific strengths] What needs to improve: [the real gap — be specific] My relationship with this person: [manager / peer / direct report] Tone: [direct / supportive / formal] Make it actionable. Each piece of feedback should include what to do differently, not just what was wrong.
Feedback
Evaluate a Plan
Evaluate this plan against these criteria: - Is the goal clear and measurable? - Are the steps specific enough to act on? - Are there gaps, dependencies, or unrealistic assumptions? - Does it account for likely resistance or failure modes? Be a thoughtful critic, not just a checklist. Tell me what you would change and why. Plan: [paste here]
Feedback
Self-Assessment Prompt
Help me write a honest self-assessment for [performance review / year-end / project close]. Time period: [timeframe] My role: [title and main responsibilities] What I'm most proud of: [2–3 specific things] Where I fell short: [what I'd do differently] What I want to work on next: [growth area] Tone: honest, confident, and self-aware — not self-promotional or self-deprecating. Make it 3–4 short paragraphs.
Brainstorming & Ideation
Brainstorming
Generate Ideas
Generate [10–20] ideas for [the challenge / goal / opportunity]. Context: [brief description] Constraints: [budget, timeline, audience, or technical limits] Don't filter — include unconventional ideas alongside practical ones. After the full list, flag the 3 you think are most promising and explain why.
Brainstorming
Challenge My Assumptions
I'm working on [project / plan / approach]. List the assumptions I'm probably making — ones I haven't questioned because they seem obvious. Then for each: what would be true if the assumption were wrong, and what would I do differently? My current approach: [describe]
Brainstorming
Analogies and Frameworks
I'm trying to solve [problem]. What analogous situations from other industries or domains have tackled something similar? For each analogy: describe how they solved it, what the key insight was, and how that maps to my situation. Give me 3–4 distinct analogies, not just variations on the same idea.
Brainstorming
Name or Title Generator
Generate name / title options for [product / project / initiative / document]. It should convey: [key qualities — e.g., speed, trust, simplicity, boldness] Audience: [who will hear or see it] Tone: [professional / punchy / descriptive / memorable] Avoid: [anything that's off-limits — acronyms, competitor names, jargon, etc.] Give 10 options across different styles. For each, add one sentence on why it works.
Learning & Explanation
Learning
Teach Me This
Teach me [topic / skill / concept] from scratch. My background: [what I already know, what I don't] How I learn best: [examples / analogies / step-by-step / use cases] Goal: [what I need to be able to do with this knowledge] Don't just define things — help me understand how the pieces connect. Check my understanding at the end with 2–3 questions I should be able to answer.
Learning
Build a Learning Plan
Create a learning plan for [topic / skill]. My current level: [beginner / some exposure / intermediate] Time available: [X hours per week for Y weeks] My goal: [what I want to be able to do at the end] Structure it as: weekly focus areas, specific things to read or practice, and milestones to check progress. Prioritize applied practice over passive consumption.
Learning
FAQ Generator
Generate a FAQ for [topic / product / process / policy]. Audience: [who will be asking these questions] Format: question followed by a direct, plain-language answer (2–4 sentences each) Include: the questions people actually ask, including ones they might be embarrassed to ask Source material (if any): [paste here or describe the topic]
Learning
Socratic Challenge
I think I understand [topic / concept / approach]. Challenge my understanding. Ask me 5 questions that probe whether I actually understand it — not surface recall, but the harder questions about edge cases, trade-offs, and underlying reasoning. After I answer each one, tell me what I got right, what I missed, and what the deeper insight is.
