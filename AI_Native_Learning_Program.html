Module 1
How AI Actually Works
Understand what large language models are, how they generate text, and why they sometimes get things wrong — no math required.
LLMsTokensHallucinations
⏱ 3 hrs
What you'll learn
  • What tokens are and why they matter for cost and quality
  • How LLMs predict text (no math — just the intuition)
  • Why AI hallucinates and how to spot it
  • Key vocabulary: model, context window, temperature, fine-tuning
  • Differences between Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot
Key Concepts
TokensNot words — AI breaks text into sub-word pieces. "tokenization" = ~3 tokens. Context limits and costs are measured in tokens. Long outputs cost more and may get cut off.
Next-Token PredictionLLMs don't "think." They predict the statistically most-likely next word given everything before it. This is why they're fluent but not always accurate.
Context WindowThe AI's working memory. Claude's is 200,000 tokens (~150k words). When full, the model forgets the oldest content. Longer conversations = less "memory" available for earlier content.
TemperatureControls randomness. 0 = deterministic, precise, repeatable. 1+ = creative, varied, surprising. Use low temperature for facts and data; high for brainstorming and drafting.
HallucinationWhen the model predicts plausible-sounding text that is factually wrong. Most common for obscure facts, specific numbers, and citations. Always verify high-stakes claims independently.

When to Use Which Tool

ChatGPTBest for: broad general tasks, image generation (DALL-E), voice mode, plugin ecosystem. Avoid for: very long documents — its context window is smaller.

⏱ 4 hrs
What you'll learn

  • How to give AI the right context (who you are, the situation, the goal)
  • Specifying output format: bullets, tables, emails, slides outlines
  • Iterating on outputs: how to refine rather than restart
  • Setting tone: formal, casual, expert, persuasive
  • Negative instructions: what NOT to include
The ROLE-TASK-FORMAT-CONTEXT Framework
RoleTell the AI who to be. Sets expertise, tone, and perspective. Example: "You are a senior operations manager at a global manufacturing company with expertise in supply chain risk..."
TaskBe explicit about what you want done. Vague tasks = vague outputs. Example: "...analyze this supplier contract and identify the top 3 risks related to delivery terms, penalties, and termination clauses."
FormatSpecify the output structure before the AI writes anything. Example: "...Output as: (1) Risk summary in bullet form, (2) Recommended action for each risk, (3) Clauses to renegotiate highlighted."
ContextGive the AI the background it needs to calibrate. Example: "...The audience is our CFO. We are renewing this contract next month and currently have a strong negotiating position — another vendor has quoted 15% lower."
Before vs. AfterBefore: "Write a status update." — After: "You are a project manager. Write a RAG status update for [project]. Format: Status (R/A/G + 1 sentence reason), Achievements (3 bullets), Blockers (2 bullets with owner), Next week (2 bullets). Audience: VP-level, no jargon. Max 150 words." The second version reliably produces something usable on the first try.
Practice exercise
  • Take a real work task (e.g., "write a project update email"). Write a minimal prompt, see the output, then improve the prompt using ROLE-TASK-FORMAT-CONTEXT. Compare the difference.

⏱ 3 hrs
What you'll learn

  • How to verify AI outputs — fact-checking techniques
  • Understanding AI bias and how it affects outputs
  • Your company's likely AI use policy (what to check for)
  • When to rely on AI and when to use your own judgment

Safety Checklists
Never include in AI prompts
  • Full names combined with contact details, addresses, or ID numbers
  • Passwords, API keys, authentication tokens, or access credentials
  • Client company names paired with confidential financial or strategic details
  • Unreleased product roadmaps, pricing strategies, or M&A information
  • Employee salary, performance review data, or HR records
  • Privileged legal communications or attorney work product
  • Medical records or protected health information
Always verify before acting on AI output
  • Specific statistics and percentages — check the original source
  • Named quotes attributed to real people
  • Legal, regulatory, or compliance claims
  • Financial projections and market size figures
  • Medical or clinical guidance
  • Historical dates, names, and event details
  • Any citation — AI frequently fabricates plausible-sounding references

Module 7
Advanced Prompting Techniques
Go beyond basic prompts with techniques that unlock dramatically better AI outputs: chain-of-thought, few-shot examples, and persona prompting.
Chain-of-thoughtFew-shotPersonas
⏱ 3 hrs
What you'll learn
  • Chain-of-thought: "Think step by step before answering"
  • Few-shot prompting: showing examples of what you want
  • Role/persona prompting: "Act as a senior strategy consultant"
  • System prompts: setting persistent context and rules
  • Iterative refinement: building on outputs across turns
  • XML tags and structured prompts for complex tasks
Technique Quick Reference
Chain-of-ThoughtTrigger step-by-step reasoning by adding "Think through this step by step before answering" or "First identify the key factors, then weigh the trade-offs, then recommend." Dramatically improves accuracy on complex analysis, decisions, and troubleshooting. The model shows its work, which also makes errors easier to spot.
Few-Shot PromptingShow 2–3 examples of the output format you want before asking for the real thing. "Here are two risk items formatted the way I want: [examples]. Now generate 5 more for this project: [description]." The model mirrors the examples almost exactly — the more specific your examples, the more precise the output.
Persona Prompting"Act as a skeptical CFO reviewing this business case. Challenge the financial assumptions, identify what the board will push back on, and list 3 questions I should be prepared to answer." Specific personas with a defined adversarial or expert stance produce dramatically sharper outputs than generic ones.
Structured XML PromptsFor complex multi-part prompts, organize with tags: <context>background</context> <task>what to do</task> <constraints>what to avoid</constraints> <format>output structure</format>. Reduces ambiguity on long prompts and prevents the model from blending sections together.

Frameworks & Templates
BATNA — Know This Before Every NegotiationYour Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement is what you'll do if talks fail. A strong BATNA gives you real leverage; knowing theirs gives you even more. Never walk into a negotiation without having defined yours. The party who needs the deal less has the power.
5-Step Vendor Evaluation1. Requirements fit — must-haves vs. nice-to-haves coverage score. 2. Total cost of ownership — license + implementation + training + ongoing support, not just the headline price. 3. Reference checks — talk to 2 current customers in a similar industry, ask what they'd do differently. 4. Contract terms — SLAs, termination clauses, data ownership, annual price escalation caps. 5. Vendor health — financial stability, product roadmap investment, support response time commitments.
Negotiation Prep Prompt"I'm negotiating with [vendor type] for [product/service]. My situation: [context — budget, timeline, alternatives, how much I need this]. Identify: 5 negotiation leverage points I have, 3 concessions I can offer that cost me little but matter to them, 3 contract terms I should push back on, and 5 questions I must get answered before signing."
Stakeholder Communication RulesExecutives want outcomes and risks — not process details. Always lead with the recommendation, then the reasoning (not the other way around). Frame problems as decisions needing to be made, not just status updates. Bad news lands better when paired with a clear proposed solution and a specific ask.

What Makes an AI Agent
ToolsAgents can take real-world actions, not just generate text: search the web, read and write files, run code, send emails, fill forms, click buttons. The set of tools available defines what the agent can actually accomplish. No tools = just a chatbot.
MemoryShort-term: the current conversation context (what the agent can "see" right now). Long-term: external storage the agent reads and writes across sessions. Without long-term memory, every new session starts from zero — the agent forgets everything.
PlanningBreaking a complex goal into sub-tasks, sequencing them correctly, and adapting the plan when a sub-task fails or produces an unexpected result. Good agents surface their plan before acting — that's your chance to redirect before they do anything irreversible.
Action LoopsExecute an action → observe the result → decide what to do next → execute again. This "sense-plan-act" loop is what lets agents handle multi-step tasks that require adapting to real-world outcomes rather than just following a fixed script.
Choosing Your Level of OversightFull automation: agent acts without review — use only for low-stakes, easily reversible tasks. Approval-gated: agent proposes each action, you confirm before execution — for medium-stakes tasks. Human executes: agent drafts, you take the final action — for high-stakes or irreversible tasks. Match oversight level to consequence level.

The Team Adoption Playbook
Stage 1: Awareness (Week 1)Action: Run a 15-minute live demo using your team's actual work — not a generic example. Let them watch you do something they recognize. Goal: at least 3 people say "I didn't know it could do that."
Stage 2: First Use (Weeks 2–3)Action: Assign each person one specific, low-stakes task to try with AI this week. Give them the prompt template to start with. Goal: everyone has at least one real output to show. Don't make it optional.
Stage 3: Habit Building (Weeks 4–8)Action: Embed AI into one recurring team workflow — weekly reports, meeting prep, status updates. Make it the default, not the exception. Goal: at least one person uses AI unprompted without being reminded.
Stage 4: Advocacy (Week 8+)Action: Ask your best adopter to run a 15-minute show-and-tell for the team. Peer-to-peer teaching is more effective and more credible than top-down mandates. Goal: AI becomes part of how the team talks about their work.
Handling Common Resistance
  • "It's not accurate enough" → Show them how to verify outputs; accuracy improves with better prompts and the right task types
  • "It'll replace my job" → Reframe: AI takes over the tedious parts and frees you for judgment-intensive work that actually matters
  • "I don't have time to learn" → Show a 3-minute workflow that saves 30 minutes/week — let the ROI speak for itself

The ROI Framework
The Core Formula(Minutes saved per use × Uses per week × 52 weeks) ÷ 60 = Annual hours saved. Annual hours saved × Fully loaded hourly cost = Annual dollar value per person. Multiply by team size for total organizational impact.
Worked ExampleTask: drafting weekly status reports. Before AI: 45 min. With AI: 10 min. Saved: 35 min/week. Annual hours saved: (35×52)÷60 = 30.3 hours. At $80/hr loaded cost = $2,427/person/year. Across a 10-person team = $24,270/year. From one workflow change.
What to Track (Quantitative)
  • Time per task: before AI vs. with AI — use a stopwatch for 2 weeks to get real numbers
  • Throughput: volume of tasks completed per week before and after
  • Error/rework rate: how often AI output required major correction or complete redo
  • Adoption rate: % of team using AI for eligible tasks at least once per week
What to Track (Qualitative)
  • Employee satisfaction: did people find their work more or less fulfilling?
  • Output quality: did stakeholders or customers notice an improvement?
  • Confidence: are people more willing to take on complex or unfamiliar tasks?

Your AI Radar System
Tier 1: Primary Sources (Monthly)Official release announcements from model providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind). These tell you what new capabilities actually exist — don't rely on secondhand summaries. Spend 20 minutes once a month scanning provider blogs and changelogs directly.
Tier 2: Practitioner Synthesis (Weekly)Find 1–2 people who translate AI news into business use cases — not hype, not doom, just "here's how this changes what you can do." Follow them consistently. Skim one piece of their content per week. Goal: 10 minutes of signal, no noise.
Tier 3: Your Own Experiments (Always)The most valuable learning is doing. Run at least one new AI experiment per week, even if it's only 10 minutes trying a new prompt style. Document what works and what doesn't in your personal prompt library. This tier compounds — your experiments become your competitive advantage.
What to Filter Out (Noise)
